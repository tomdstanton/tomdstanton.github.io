Following graduation of my BSc (Hons.) in Biomedical Science, I spent 3 years at Public Health England, Colindale on the BSAC Resistance Surveillance Program and the Genomic Services and Development Unit. I then moved to Edinburgh to undertake a Biomedical Science PhD elucidating the mechanisms of resistance to the novel antibiotic, cefiderocol, in the Gram-negative, oppourtunistic pathogen, Klebsiella pneumoniae. After completing my PhD, I moved back to London and began a Gates Foundation funded project with Prof. Kathryn Holt and Dr. Kelly Wyres (Monash, Australia) on Klebsiella capsule seroepidemiology for vaccine development.

I use computational biology approaches to investigate capsule locus diversity and genomic epidemiology of Klebsiella pneumoniae, a World Health Organization priority pathogen. K. pneumoniae has been listed as a priority target for development of novel control strategies due to growing rates of multi-drug resistant strains that are extremely difficult to treat. Its polysaccharide capsule is a key vaccine target, and genomic analysis of its capsule synthesis loci has indicated extensive capsule type diversity across the population. However, until now there has been limited serological typing data with which to fully understand the relationship between capsule genotype and phenotype, which is a key prerequisite for successful vaccine design. In collaboration with researchers at the Statens Serum Institut (Denmark), we aim to address this knowledge gap through fine-scale analysis of K. pneumoniae capsule synthesis loci and comparison to newly generated serological typing data.
